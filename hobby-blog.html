Japan’s Food Service Industry: Culture, Aesthetics, and Values

Introduction

When prepared with a skillful hand, food is an art. However, this type of art is not one that withstands time. Food is meant to be enjoyed, consumed, lest it go to waste. When food is consumed it is essentially destroyed; it cannot be reconstructed in the same way. This destruction in itself can be viewed as a step in the cycle of art. The Japanese as a culture and society value food as an art form, recognizing that the consumer should enjoy the food before its proverbial destruction. Mono no aware, which will be introduced in greater detail, is a Japanese term that encompasses this process of the life cycle of art, including its destruction.

Throughout this chapter, the Japanese aesthetics of mono no aware and kodawari will be introduced and applied to examples in context, such as Japanese tea ceremony, to provide the reader a more thorough understanding of how these aesthetics influence the food and service industry in a later chapter.

Mono No Aware

Deconstructing the Japanese aesthetic term, mono no aware, is no simple task. The term carries with it centuries of meaning and depth, but is still applicable because it remains relevant to modern Japan. More than just a term, mono no aware is a mindset: the awareness of impermanence, the appreciation of the fleeting beauty in the cycle of life. Specifically applicable in nature, mono no aware emphasizes a respect and reverence for natural beauty, both in its prime element and in its passing.

During an interview with Portland based artist, Saager Dilawri, Zero1 Magazine asked him to define mono no aware, as he has been known to say it is a key influence in his work. “It is a Japanese idea,” he states, “like a perfect notion of seeing a leaf decaying with light catching it. Letting things be in their natural state and understanding that nothing lasts forever. Time has an effect that nothing is perfect and that there is beauty in everything” (Dilawri, p. 8). Mono no aware is the awareness of impermanence verbalized.
